Doomscrolling.

What is it? Why does it happen? How to fix it?

Doomscrolling is the habit of endlessly scrolling through negative news online,
often leaving you anxious or stressed. It’s driven by our natural curiosity and fear of missing out but can harm mental health.

Why It Happens
Negativity Bias: We’re wired to focus on threats.
FOMO: The need to stay updated on bad news.
Algorithms: Platforms show sensational content to keep us engaged.
Why It’s Harmful
Causes anxiety, stress, and poor sleep.
Makes the world seem overwhelmingly negative.
How to Stop Doomscrolling
Set Limits: Schedule specific times to check the news.
Turn Off Notifications: Avoid constant interruptions.
Follow Positivity: Curate feeds for balanced content.
Practice Mindfulness: Meditate, journal, or spend time offline.

The Effects of Doomscrolling.

1. Heightened Anxiety and Stress

Constant exposure to alarming news keeps your mind in a state of alert, triggering the fight-or-flight response.
Over time, this can lead to chronic stress and an overwhelming sense of helplessness.

2. Negative Impact on Mental Health

Studies link doomscrolling to feelings of depression, loneliness, and even burnout.
The cycle of consuming distressing information can fuel pessimism and a sense of dread about the world.

3. Disrupted Sleep Patterns

Late-night scrolling can interfere with your sleep, as blue light from screens suppresses melatonin and distressing
content keeps your mind racing long after you’ve put down your device.

4. Reduced Productivity and Focus

Doomscrolling eats into your time, leaving you distracted and drained. The constant influx of negativity
can sap your energy and make it harder to concentrate on meaningful tasks.

5. A Narrowed Perspective

When you’re stuck in the doomscrolling loop, it’s easy to lose sight of positive stories and diverse perspectives,
which are crucial for a balanced understanding of the world.
